The studio takes one or two engagements at a time. It always has. Every
twelve months I think about scaling it, and every twelve months I
decide not to. This post is mostly so I can stop having the same
internal argument.

## The case for staying small

The case is simple: the work gets better when there is less of it. Not
linearly — a studio of one with one client is not as good as a studio
of one with three, because the variety helps. But past three or four
parallel engagements, attention thins out, and the kind of work I want
to do depends on attention.

I do not have anything against bigger studios. I worked at a few of
them. They are good at things small studios cannot do — multi-quarter
engagements, large teams, work that requires twenty people in a room.
That is real, and I send people there when the work calls for it.

What I want to do is different. I want to be in the room with a small
team, for a few months, and leave the room having moved something
important. That requires being available, and being available requires
being small.

## The trade

The honest trade is that I cannot help everyone who asks, and I
sometimes have to wait three months to start work I am excited about.
Both of those things hurt. But the alternative — saying yes to
everything and doing it less well — hurts more, because it hurts the
people who hired me on the assumption that I would do it well.

I would rather have a short list of people I helped seriously than a
long list of people I helped a little.

## Slow on purpose

Most of the engagements I am proud of took longer than they were
supposed to. Not because of bad scoping, but because the right answer
took a few weeks longer to arrive than the expected answer. I have
stopped apologizing for this. The expected answer is usually the
expensive one — expensive in the sense that it costs more, eighteen
months later, to undo.

So: small, deliberate, slow on purpose. If those words sound right,
the contact form is one click away.
